Speculative Designs

What is the story we tell about the invention of modern data visualization? What alternate forms could we imagine if we told that story differently?

History

     Elizabeth Palmer Peabody was born in 1804, in a town 25 miles northwest of Boston, Massachusetts. Today, she is most famous for her proximity to more well-known writers of the American Renaissance, such as Henry David Thoreau and Nathaniel Hawthorne. But Peabody had impact in her own right. The bookstore that she ran out of her home in Boston functioned as the de facto salon for the transcendentalist movement. She also served as the business manager of the transcendentalist magazine, The Dial, during its formative years. And like many women of the nineteenth century who didn’t have many career options, Peabody eventually became an educator. In fact, she is credited with starting the first kindergarten in the United States.
     This educational work was what led Peabody, in 1856, to publish her Chronological History of the United States, the source of the charts that serve as the basis for this project. The Chronological History was intended to instruct U.S. schoolchildren in how they – and their young nation – fit into what Peabody understood as the "great narrative of

time." The textbook consisted of a series of history lessons, structured so that the reader would be guided through the process of learning to understand – and, crucially, to interpret – the four chronological charts that were printed inside. The geometric charts were inspired by a system developed earlier in the century by a Polish educator and military general, Józef Bem. It was Bem's idea to display the major events in (Western) world history as a series of color-coded grids. In Bem's method, Peabody saw a way "to do for the science of history what maps do for that of geography." She strongly believed in the power of the sense of sight, what she called the "natural memorizer," to impress history upon the mind.
     In fact, in 1850, several years before penning her own textbook, Peabody published a translation of Bem's original book. It took the form of a list of events, interspersed with commentary. In that volume, students were directed to consult larger versions of the charts, which Peabody described as Speaking Charts" – sold separately, of course – from which the teacher could instruct. With the purchase of

Bem's volume, Peabody also included a set of blank charts – one set for each student – so that they could internalize the method for themselves.
     As should now be clear, Peabody's project is both interpretive and interactive – two concepts that loom large in our culture today. In recreating her charts for the web, our hope is to draw users attention to the possibilities of both for visualization today. // LK to here 5/15/16
